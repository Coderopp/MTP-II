\begin{center}
    \Large \textbf{\uppercase{Abstract}}
\end{center}

\vspace{3\baselineskip}

\noindent
Quick commerce routing in dense Indian cities is usually optimized for speed, even though the operational environment is shaped by congestion, crash risk, and narrow residential streets. This thesis reformulates that dispatch problem as the Safety-Aware Vehicle Routing Problem with Time Windows (SA-VRPTW). The proposed model minimizes a primary service-quality objective consisting of early-arrival waiting and an exponential soft-time-window lateness penalty, while treating route safety and residential-street exposure as epsilon-constraints rather than weighted-sum objectives. The street network is built from OpenStreetMap, travel times are inflated with a Bureau of Public Roads congestion model, and per-edge crash probabilities are attached using a reproducible Bengaluru and Indian-city Accident Surrogate Model (BASM). BASM combines aggregate road-safety statistics from the Ministry of Road Transport and Highways, directionality from Mohan et al.\ on arterial versus non-arterial safety, and graph-local OpenStreetMap proxies such as intersection centrality, signal density, and crossing density. The retained single-brand experiment design covers five cities---Bengaluru, Delhi, Gurugram, Mumbai, and Pune---because the committed Blinkit snapshot used in this study contains no Hyderabad placemarks. Three solution approaches are retained in the final architecture: an exact mixed-integer linear program with piecewise-linear soft-time-window approximation, a giant-tour genetic algorithm with depot-aware split decoding, and an adaptive large neighbourhood search. A post-hoc Monte Carlo evaluator quantifies expected crash events and behavioural compliance sensitivity without contaminating the optimization objective. The current branch delivers the corrected formulation, reproducible calibration, executable software pipeline, and thesis-ready methodology; full figure generation and the final five-city experiment grid are defined as the next empirical step.
