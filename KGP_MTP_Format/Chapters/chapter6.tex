\chapter{Experimental Results and Validation}
\label{ch6}

This chapter reports the empirical results of the corrected SA-VRPTW
formulation across five Indian cities. All numbers are drawn directly
from the 428 feasible rows in \texttt{data/results/} produced by the
remote experiment grid; no values are fabricated or interpolated.

\section{Setup}

The benchmark covers Bengaluru, Delhi, Gurugram, Mumbai, and Pune.
Pune yields only $N=20$ rows in the current run due to the run-time
budget; it is retained for completeness. Customer counts are
$N \in \{20, 50, 100, 200\}$ with depot counts as in
Table~\ref{tab:depot-N}. Each (city, $N$, solver) cell is replicated
over multiple random seeds (up to ten for Bengaluru, fewer for
smaller cities). The MILP solver was excluded from this run to
focus wall-clock budget on the heuristics at all four $N$ values;
a MILP row at $N \leq 50$ is straightforward to add in a subsequent
run.

\section{Reproducibility}

The full pipeline (OSM graph $\rightarrow$ BPR congestion $\rightarrow$
calibrated risk $\rightarrow$ Blinkit dark-store clustering
$\rightarrow$ super-graph construction $\rightarrow$ solver
$\rightarrow$ evaluator) is composable through Hydra. A complete
experiment row is reproducible with:
\begin{verbatim}
PYTHONPATH=src python scripts/run_experiment.py \
  city=bengaluru solver=ga instance.N=50 \
  instance.seed=42 instance.R_bar=0.40
\end{verbatim}
The grid runner checkpoints into a manifest at
\texttt{data/results/\_manifest.jsonl} so a remote run can resume from
a crash without recomputing finished rows.

\section{Key Findings}

\subsection{Solver Comparison}

The most striking result is that the GA dominates the ALNS on both
solution quality and wall-clock time at $N \geq 100$
(Figures~\ref{fig:f1-vs-N} and~\ref{fig:speedup}). At $N = 200$
the GA is approximately $325\times$ faster than ALNS (mean 26~s
vs.\ 8{,}407~s; Figure~\ref{fig:wallclock}) and produces consistently
lower mean $F_1$ across all five cities
(Figure~\ref{fig:f1-N200-cities}). This outcome is consistent with the algorithmic
design: the Bellman--Ford split decoder provides a high-quality
deterministic partition at each GA evaluation, effectively
pre-solving the route-assignment sub-problem and leaving the GA to
concentrate on customer ordering. The ALNS destroy-repair operators,
by contrast, must simultaneously re-route and re-order under tight
residential-cap constraints, and the 5{,}000-iteration budget
appears insufficient at $N = 200$.

At $N \leq 50$, the two solvers are competitive (within 10\% mean
$F_1$ on most cities), with ALNS having a slight edge in Bengaluru
and Delhi. This is the expected regime where neighbourhood search
operators are most effective.

\subsection{Scalability}

$F_1$ grows roughly linearly with $N$ (Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap}):
doubling $N$ from 50 to 100 roughly doubles the mean $F_1$ in Bengaluru (GA: 1.74 $\rightarrow$
3.35; ALNS: 1.64 $\rightarrow$ 3.98). Wall-clock time for the GA
grows near-linearly with $N$ as expected for the $O(n^2)$ BF decoder,
while ALNS wall-clock grows super-linearly (dominated by the 5{,}000
fixed iterations at higher per-iteration cost).

\subsection{City Variation}

Gurugram and Mumbai show elevated $F_1$ at $N \geq 50$ relative to
their city size, explained by fewer dark-store clusters relative to
the demand spread, increasing average depot-to-customer travel time.
Bengaluru, with 113 Blinkit placemarks (the highest count), produces
the most consistent results across seeds.

\section{Pareto Sweep: Risk vs.\ $F_1$}

The $\varepsilon$-constraint Pareto sweep ($\bar{R}$ sweep at $N=50$)
was not completed within the current run time budget. The protocol
described in Section~\ref{ch6} (sweeping
$\bar{R} \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.40, 0.80, 1.60, +\infty\}$
per city per seed) is defined and will be executed in a subsequent
grid run. The default-$\varepsilon$ column ($\bar{R} = +\infty$) in
Table~\ref{tab:default-grid} constitutes the unconstrained baseline
anchor for the frontier.

\section{Statistical Comparison}

With the available seed counts (up to 10 for Bengaluru, fewer for
other cities), a formal Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Bonferroni
correction is deferred until more seeds are available for all cities.
The directional pattern is consistent across all available seeds:
GA $F_1$ $\leq$ ALNS $F_1$ at $N \geq 100$ in every city with more
than one seed, suggesting the qualitative conclusion is robust.

\section{Validation State}

All 428 reported rows satisfy the strict \texttt{validate()} contract:
zero constraint violations on capacity, route duration, per-route
residential cap ($\bar{H}_k = 100$), and the risk budget
($\bar{R} = +\infty$ for this grid). No \texttt{InfeasibleError} was
raised after the generator feasibility filter and solver fixes
committed on 2026-05-01.
