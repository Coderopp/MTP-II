\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Quick commerce has reconfigured urban logistics around extreme response times. Orders are released in small batches from dark stores, riders are assigned under tight promised arrival times, and dispatch systems repeatedly trade between route length, traffic, and reliability. In practice, these systems are usually optimized for speed first and safety later. That design choice is operationally convenient but ethically incomplete in dense Indian cities, where the fastest route may coincide with the highest arterial exposure, the most aggressive congestion, and the strongest pressure to cut through residential streets.

The classical vehicle routing problem, beginning with \citet{dantzig1959} and extended by the vehicle routing problem with time windows literature summarized by \citet{solomon1987}, \citet{desrochers1992}, and \citet{toth2002}, was not built for this setting. Traditional formulations minimize distance, time, or fleet size under hard feasibility constraints. For quick commerce, those objectives are too narrow. Delivery quality depends on promised and realized arrival times, while public risk depends on the streets through which the algorithm repeatedly sends riders. A safety-aware routing model therefore has to represent not only timeliness but also where the traffic burden is shifted and how route choice changes under risk budgets.

India's road safety burden makes the problem concrete rather than hypothetical. The Ministry of Road Transport and Highways reported 4,61,312 road accidents, 1,68,491 fatalities, and 4,43,366 injuries in 2022 \citep{morth2022}. The same report shows that fatalities are disproportionately concentrated on higher-order roads despite their relatively small share of total network length. At the same time, Mohan, Bangdiwala, and Villaveces argue that a larger share of highways and main arterial roads is associated with higher urban fatality rates, whereas denser junction structure is associated with lower mortality \citep{mohan2017}. These observations suggest that routing should not treat all street kilometres as operationally interchangeable.

\section{Problem Statement}

Current q-commerce routing engines rarely expose an explicit mathematical mechanism for limiting per-route crash exposure or for protecting local streets from systematic cut-through traffic. Weighted-sum formulations also obscure trade-offs by collapsing incompatible quantities into one scalar. A route may appear optimal simply because a chosen weight vector hides the safety cost. The central problem of this thesis is therefore to formulate a dispatch model in which service quality remains the primary optimization target, but crash-survival risk and residential exposure are enforced as explicit, auditable constraints.

\section{Research Objectives}

This thesis develops the \textbf{Safety-Aware Vehicle Routing Problem with Time Windows (SA-VRPTW)} with the following objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Formulate a canonical objective in which service quality is measured by early-arrival waiting and an exponential soft-time-window lateness penalty instead of raw travel time alone.
    \item Represent safety through a per-route crash-survival budget and represent neighbourhood protection through fleet-wide and route-level residential-road caps.
    \item Build a reproducible data pipeline that combines OpenStreetMap road networks, BPR congestion inflation, and BASM edge-level crash probabilities calibrated from public sources.
    \item Implement three solution approaches consistent with the same formulation: exact MILP, a depot-aware genetic algorithm, and adaptive large neighbourhood search.
    \item Define a five-city empirical protocol for Bengaluru, Delhi, Gurugram, Mumbai, and Pune, with post-hoc crash and behavioural Monte Carlo evaluation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis Organization}

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows. Chapter~2 reviews the literature on VRPTW optimization, safety-aware routing, and behavioural logistics. Chapter~3 presents the corrected SA-VRPTW formulation. Chapter~4 documents the data pipeline, including congestion and BASM calibration. Chapter~5 describes the exact and metaheuristic solution approaches. Chapter~6 defines the experimental protocol and the current validation status of the software pipeline. Chapter~7 concludes with contributions, limitations, and the immediate next empirical steps.
